About the Homeschool Petition (2019)

 

Mr. Silus Shikwekwe Were is a homeschooling parent based in Malava Kakamega country. He and his three children were arrested one evening in February 2019 and forced to spend the night in a police cell. His Children were arrested, interrogated and incarcerated by the police without the involvement of an adult nor the supervision of a Children's officer.

He was subsequently charged for negligence because his children were ‘not attending school,’ and his case is still being prosecuted. This is not an isolated case. In the last few years, the homeschooling fraternity in Kenya has witnessed a number of similar cases where their members are persecuted and harassed by the state; their only crime being, their choice to educate their children from home. Together with Mr. Silas and other homeschooling families, we are now petitioning the Constitutional Division of the High Court of Kenya in Nairobi and in Kakamega for a determination on whether homeschooling meets the Constitutional requirement for basic education and whether it should be classified as an offense.

MCSE boot camps have its supporters and its detractors. It is estimated that over 1500 families in Kenya have successfully homeschooled over a period of more than 30 years. However, when the Constitution of Kenya, 2010 was promulgated and basic education was recognized as a mandatory requirement that should be guaranteed by the state, it became necessary for legislation to safeguard this. The Basic Education Act of 2013 was subsequently enacted, and it expressly underscored ‘the right of every child to free and compulsory basic education.’ It also required every parent to ‘ensure that the child attends regularly as a pupil at a school or such other institution as may be authorized and prescribed by the Cabinet Secretary.’ While noble in its mission, the act suddenly placed many families practicing home education at risk of being criminals.

Meanwhile, other instruments of Law both locally and internationally places the responsibility on a parent to provide for the education of the child and defines the right to give parental guidance in Education, religious, moral, social cultural and other values.(Ref The Children’s Act, 2001, Article 18 of the Conventions of the Rights of the Child (to which Kenya is a signatory) ‘Parents have a prior right to choose the kind of education that shall be given to their children.’ [emphasis added]

Many countries across the globe recognize homeschooling as a fundamental part of the education process, and Kenya had no contention with homeschooling until the Basic Education Act (2013) was passed. There is now an opportunity for this to be clarified by the Constitutional division of the High Court.

Precipitated by the aforegoing, we have since petitioned the Constitutional Division of the High Court of Kenya under Petition no 236 of 2019 to test whether Homeschooling meets the constitutional requirement of Right to Education.
